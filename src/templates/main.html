Forest fires in Indonesia occur every year

The country produces the most pulpwood, timber and palm oil in the world and vast tracts of land are set alight to clear the way for their production. Since 2011 the government has imposed a moratorium to protect 43m hectares of forest and peatlands but fires still occur on this land

The fires are illegal

The Indonesian government has started to pressure big palm oil and pulpwood companies like Asia Pulp and Paper to eliminate the practice from their supply chains. Despite this, some of the densest concentrations of fires occur on corporate concession land

An acrid haze enveloped Indonesia and neighbouring countries as the fires spread

It grounded flights, closed schools and reduced visibility to 30 metres in some areas while also severely affecting public health

The haze contained high levels of pollutants such as carbon monoxide

At the height of the crisis, levels of six pollutants were recorded six times higher than a baseline measure normally considered hazardous on the Pollutants Standards Index. More than half a million people were treated for respiratory infections and at least 19 people died

The carbon dioxide emitted by the fires now exceeds the annual emissions of many major countries, such as the UK and Japan

Environmentalists have accused the government of dodging their responsibilities ahead of crunch UN climate change talks in Paris.
